Recursive Learning Models

The machines are grading their own homework now.

And it turns out they're getting better at it than we are. Recursive learning -- AI systems that improve by studying their own outputs -- is quietly becoming the most consequential development in machine intelligence since the transformer architecture.

This site exists because the topic deserves better than a Twitter thread. Start with fundamentals, or dive into what's actually working.

The thesis

Why this matters more than you think

For fifty years, AI improved because humans got better at building it. Better architectures, better data, better training recipes. The human was always in the loop.

That loop is closing.

In January 2025, DeepSeek released R1 -- a model trained with pure reinforcement learning, no human demonstrations. It taught itself chain-of-thought reasoning from scratch. A few months later, Google DeepMind shipped AlphaEvolve, an agent that uses LLMs to design better algorithms. The algorithms it designs are then used to improve the next generation of LLMs.

Read that again. The output of the system becomes the input to its own improvement.

This is what we mean by recursive learning. Not a vague sci-fi concept about superintelligence. A concrete, measurable, already-happening shift in how AI systems get better.

The punchline

This isn't speculative

Every major lab is betting on recursive learning. OpenAI's o-series models use test-time compute scaling -- reasoning harder at inference rather than just training bigger. Anthropic's constitutional AI uses the model's own judgment to align itself. Google's AlphaProof taught itself to solve olympiad-level mathematics.

The question isn't whether recursive learning works. It does. The question is how far it goes -- and what happens when the improvement curves start compounding.

Eric Schmidt, Dario Amodei, and Sam Altman have all pointed to recursive self-improvement as the capability that makes everything else possible. Not AGI in the abstract. This, specifically.

New York Times, January 2026: "One new start-up is called Recursive with an 'e.' Another is called Ricursive with an 'i.' They are trying to do the same thing: Build artificial intelligence that can improve itself without the help of humans."
